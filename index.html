Garlic is a widely used ingredient in many popular dishes, and although we most commonly see bunches of bulbs or pre-minced jars in the grocery store, many people are unfamiliar with the way garlic actually grows!

Garlic Crash Course

Garlic is a flowering plant that is part of the allium genus. Allium is actually the Latin word for garlic! The English word "garlic" is derived from Old English, garlÄ“ac, meaning gar (spear) and leek, as a 'spear-shaped leek'. Close relatives of garlic plants include onions, chives, shallots, and leek. These plants are known for their fragrant (and often considered unpleasant) smell, as well as their wide variety of use in foods from around the world! The part most commonly eaten is the bulb, although the flower and leaves are also edible. Garlic has been a staple of global cuisine since ancient times. The bulbs typically yield around 10 to 20 cloves. When the cloves are crushed, they release the chemical compound "allicin," which is partially responsible for garlic's strong fragrance. Allicin is often cited as being a natural antibiotic, which you may hear from people who eat raw garlic every morning. Research on the potential health benefits from allicin have been inconclusive.

Garlic Flower Facts

  1. Garlic flowers are typically a light purple or white color when in bloom.
  2. Garlic flowers are edible, and taste more mild than the garlic bulb.
  3. In the novel "Dracula" by Bram Stoker, garlic flowers are used to ward off vampires, not garlic bulbs!
